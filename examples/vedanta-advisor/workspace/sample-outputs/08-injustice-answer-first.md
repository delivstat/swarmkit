## Response

I hear you — that feeling of pouring your whole self into something and watching others glide past on connections you don't have is genuinely painful. It's not "all in your head." The texts take this seriously.

**The Gita's answer is direct: shift your claim from the *fruit* to the *action itself*.**

Krishna tells Arjuna:

> **कर्मण्येवाधिकारस्ते मा फलेषु कदाचन |**
> *"Your right is to action alone, never to its fruits."* (Gita 2.47)

And immediately after:

> **योगस्थः कुरु कर्माणि सङ्गं त्यक्त्वा धनञ्जय |**
> **सिद्ध्यसिद्ध्योः समो भूत्वा समत्वं योग उच्यते ||**
> *"Be established in yoga, perform actions abandoning attachment, O Dhananjaya. Be equipoised in success and failure — this evenness is called yoga."* (Gita 2.48)

This isn't about pretending injustice doesn't exist. It's about recognizing that **the result of any action depends on five factors**, only one of which is your effort. Krishna lists them: the body/instruments, the doer, the various senses, the effort, and the unseen *daiva* — the forces beyond your control (Gita 18.14). Recognition, timing, connections, luck — these are real variables. When you stake your peace on controlling them, you hand your happiness to circumstances.

---

**Let me tell you why this matters, through the story of Karna.**

Karna was arguably the most skilled warrior of his age. He trained harder than anyone, mastered archery through sheer will, and proved his excellence again and again. But he was born to a charioteer — or so the world believed — and the doors of recognition kept slamming in his face. Drona refused to teach him. The Kaurava princes mocked his origins. Even when he became a king, the stain of "low birth" followed him.

His bitterness became his defining feature. Every slight calcified into resentment. He made alliances not from principle but from grievance — Duryodhana embraced him not for who he was, but to use him against the Pandavas. And in the end, Karna's finest qualities — his generosity, his courage, his loyalty — were all consumed by the fire of "I was denied what I deserved."

The Mahabharata doesn't say Karna was wrong about the injustice. He *was* wronged. But it shows us that **bitterness is a second wound we choose**, and it distorts everything — our judgment, our relationships, and ultimately our own excellence.

---

**A practical reflection:**

The next time that familiar heat rises — watching someone less capable get promoted, praised, platformed — try this: ask yourself, *"If no one ever sees this, is the work still worth doing?"* Not as resignation, but as clarity. If the answer is yes, you've found something stable to stand on. If the answer is no, that's useful information too — it may mean you've been working for validation rather than for the thing itself.

You don't have to stop wanting recognition. But the Gita suggests making your *primary* reward the integrity of the action itself. Everything else — recognition, advancement, justice — becomes a welcome bonus, not a debt the world owes you.

What kind of work are you pouring yourself into? And is there a way to do it that would feel meaningful even if the spotlight never turns your way?
