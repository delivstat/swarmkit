## Response

I am so sorry for your loss. A month is no time at all — and feeling like a part of you died with her is not weakness; it is the measure of how deeply you loved.

The Gita's answer to this is not to tell you to stop feeling. It is to say that the person you mourn is not truly "gone" in the way your grief imagines. When Arjuna stood on the battlefield, weeping, his limbs trembling, refusing to fight because he could not bear the thought of losing those he loved, Krishna did not dismiss his sorrow. He met it with a radical reframing:

**"You grieve for those who should not be grieved for. The wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead."** (Gita 2:11)

**"For certain is death for the born, and certain is birth for the dead; therefore, over the inevitable you should not grieve."** (Gita 2:27)

**"The Self is never born and never dies. It is not killed when the body is killed."** (Gita 2:20)

What does this mean for you? Not that your pain is wrong — but that your mother is not the perishable body you last saw. The love between you, the part of you that feels "dead" now — that connection was never physical. It does not end where the body ends.

Let me tell you why through a story. In the Mahabharata, there is a moment called the Yaksha Prashna. Yudhishthira's four brothers have fallen dead by a lake, and a divine being appears to test him. Among the questions asked is this: "What is the greatest wonder in the world?" Yudhishthira answers: "Day after day, countless creatures go to the abode of death, yet those who remain behind think they are immortal. This is the greatest wonder."

We live as if those we love will always be here — and when they are not, the shock is unbearable because we had quietly promised ourselves eternity. Your grief is, in part, the collapse of that promise. But it is also proof that something real existed — a bond that death cannot touch.

There is another moment in that same story. When the Yaksha offers to revive only one brother, Yudhishthira chooses Nakula — not the strongest warrior, but the son of his other mother, Madri. He says: "I was born to Kunti, but Madri is also my mother. If I am to live, one of her sons must live too." Even in the face of death, his first thought was of his mothers. The love of a child for a mother, and a mother for a child, runs deeper than the body. That is what remains.

**A practical reflection:** Your mother lives now in the way you carry her forward — not as a ghost to be chained to, but as the part of your heart she shaped. What would she want for you? What strength did she give you that you can now offer to the world in her name?

You do not need to "move on." You need to move *with* — carrying her with you, not as a weight, but as the love that made you who you are.
