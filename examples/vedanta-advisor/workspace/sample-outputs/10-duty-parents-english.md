## Response

I hear you — that guilt is real, and it comes from love. You don't want to hurt the people who raised you. But here's what the Gita says about this exact tension:

**The texts say: follow your own nature, even imperfectly, rather than another's path, even well.**

Krishna tells Arjuna: *"Better is one's own duty, though devoid of merit, than the duty of another well discharged. Better is death in one's own duty; the duty of another is fraught with fear."* (Bhagavad Gita 3:35)

And again: *"One's own duty, though defective, is superior to another's duty well performed. He who does the duty ordained by his own nature incurs no sin."* (Bhagavad Gita 18:47)

**Why this matters for you:**

The word here is *svadharma* — not "duty" in the sense of obeying your parents' wishes, but the duty that arises from your own intrinsic nature (*svabhava*). When Arjuna stands on the battlefield, overwhelmed, he wants to drop his bow and become a beggar instead. He thinks it will be more peaceful, more virtuous. But Krishna stops him. Not because begging is bad, but because Arjuna *is a warrior*. To pretend to be a renouncer when his nature is active, courageous, and protective would be a kind of self-betrayal — what the text calls *karpanya*, a weakness disguised as virtue.

Your parents want you to be a doctor because they love you and see it as security. That love is real. But if your nature is that of an artist — if that is where your energy flows, where you lose yourself in the work, where you can offer something genuine to the world — then forcing yourself into medicine is like forcing Arjuna into a monk's robes. You may succeed on paper, but you will be performing someone else's *svadharma*.

**A practical reflection:**

This doesn't mean you need to burn bridges or declare war on your family. The Gita doesn't celebrate rebellion for its own sake. It asks: *Can you honor your parents' concern while still being honest about who you are?* Can you show them — through your seriousness about art, your plan, your stability — that your path is not escapism but *svadharma*?

The guilt you feel is the price of love. But the texts warn that the fear of disappointing others can become a prison. What would it look like to have an honest conversation with your parents not as a child asking permission, but as an adult offering clarity about the life you are actually built for?
