## Response

I hear you. That feeling — having what looks like a good life on paper but sensing something is misaligned — is one of the oldest human confusions there is. The *Bhagavad Gita* opens with exactly this.

**Arjuna's moment of doubt**

Arjuna was at the top of his game. Greatest archer of his age, respected, wealthy, about to fight a war he had prepared for his entire life. And at the moment it mattered most, standing between the two armies, he froze. He told Krishna: *I don't want this. Let them kill me instead. What is the point of kingdom or pleasure if I have to destroy my own family to get it?*

He wasn't lazy. He wasn't ungrateful. He was genuinely lost. And Krishna's response wasn't to give him a vision of a grand, cosmic purpose. It was to bring him back to his *svadharma* — his own duty, the action that arises from who he is.

Krishna said:

> **श्रेयान्स्वधर्मो विगुणः परधर्मात्स्वनुष्ठितात् ।**
> *śreyān svadharmo viguṇaḥ paradharmāt svanuṣṭhitāt*
> "Better is one's own duty, though imperfect, than the duty of another well performed." *(Gita 3.35)*

And again:

> **कर्मण्येवाधिकारस्ते मा फलेषु कदाचन ।**
> *karmaṇyevādhikāraste mā phaleṣu kadācana*
> "Your right is to action alone, never to its fruits." *(Gita 2.47)*

**What this means for your situation**

The texts don't say there is one hidden "purpose" you must discover like a treasure buried somewhere. *Svadharma* is more like the work that fits your nature, your situation, and what the moment demands of you — performed with attention, without obsessing over the outcome.

Your restlessness might be telling you one of a few things:

- **The "what" is right but the "why" has become hollow.** You have the job, but you've lost connection to the value of the work itself. The Gita's remedy here is to shift focus from the fruits (promotion, salary, status) to the quality of the action.
- **You are performing someone else's dharma.** Maybe you entered this path for family expectations, social prestige, or fear. A duty taken on from outside, even done well, creates that subtle feeling of *wrongness*.
- **Your dharma is evolving.** What was right for you at 25 may not be right at 35. Arjuna wasn't asked to be a hermit — he was asked to be a warrior *now*, because that was what the situation and his nature called for.

**A practical way to think about it**

Instead of asking "What is my grand purpose?", try asking: *What work, when I do it, makes me feel like myself?* Not necessarily happy or successful — but *like myself*. That feeling of alignment is usually closer to *svadharma* than any career aptitude test.

And if you don't know yet, the Gita gives permission to act your way into clarity. You don't need perfect certainty before you move. You need to act with sincerity, observe where you feel alive, and adjust.
