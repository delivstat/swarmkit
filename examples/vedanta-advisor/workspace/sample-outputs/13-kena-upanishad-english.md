## Response

The Kena Upanishad (also called the Talavakara Upanishad) is one of the most striking of the principal Upanishads because it doesn't just describe Brahman — it shows you why Brahman cannot be captured by description. Its central teaching is this: **Brahman is not another object in the universe to be seen or conquered; it is the invisible power by which seeing and conquering happen at all.**

## The Story of the Yaksha

The Upanishad opens with a question: *Kena?* — "By whom?" By whom is the mind directed? By whom does the eye see and the ear hear?

Then it tells a story.

Once, the gods — **Agni** (fire), **Vayu** (wind), and **Indra** (lord of the gods) — won a great victory. In their pride, they began to think: *This triumph is ours. Our power alone achieved this.*

To teach them a lesson, **Brahman appeared before them as a Yaksha** — a mysterious, luminous being whose nature they could not understand.

**Agni** approached first. "Who are you?" he demanded.

The Yaksha simply placed a blade of grass before him and said, "Burn this."

Agni tried with all his might — but he could not burn a single blade of grass. He returned to the gods, humbled.

Then **Vayu** went. The Yaksha gave him the same test. "Blow this away."

Vayu summoned all his force — but the blade of grass did not move. He too returned, defeated.

Finally, **Indra** went. But as he approached, the Yaksha vanished. In its place stood a radiant woman — **Uma Haimavati**, the daughter of the Himalayas.

Indra asked her, "What was that being?"

She replied: **"That was Brahman. It is Brahman alone by whose victory you yourselves are victorious."**

Indra alone, among all the gods, understood this truth. He became the one who "knew Brahman."

## What This Teaches About Brahman

The Kena Upanishad makes three profound points through this story:

**1. Brahman is the power behind the powers**
Agni is fire itself, yet he could not burn when Brahman withdrew its enabling power. Vayu is wind itself, yet he could not move a blade of grass. The gods are mighty, but their might is borrowed. Brahman is not a bigger god — it is the *principle* by which all gods function.

**2. Brahman cannot be grasped by force or pride**
The gods approached with ego — "I will find out what this is" — and failed. Indra succeeded only when the Yaksha disappeared and he was willing to receive instruction through Uma. The text suggests that Brahman reveals itself not to conquest, but to humility and receptive inquiry.

**3. Brahman is "the ear of the ear, the mind of the mind"**
The Upanishad famously says: *"It is the ear of the ear, the mind of the mind, the speech of speech, the breath of breath, the eye of the eye."* You cannot see Brahman with the eye, because Brahman is that by which the eye sees. You cannot think it with the mind, because it is that by which the mind thinks. It is not an object of consciousness — it is the light of consciousness itself.

## A Reflection

The Kena Upanishad doesn't give you Brahman as a doctrine to memorize. It gives you a puzzle that undoes the assumption that the highest truth can be "known" the way we know ordinary things. The gods — representing the most powerful forces in nature and psyche — are humbled by a blade of grass. What in your own life do you take credit for, without recognizing the deeper power that makes it possible?
